Not a shadowing day or research interview — a real job.

I had been consulting for companies selling into traditional industries, and noticed prospects have become less likely to offer their time for ride-alongs and research calls. I decided to go all-in and do the job for real.

## Why this industry?

I was helping a renovation company with go-to-market strategy. One of their projects was a beautiful home for a former owner of a small service business. He had built the company over two decades and adopted the vertical SaaS tools of the day from the very beginning, while competitors didn't.

The more he told me, the more I liked the sound of it: recurring revenue, specialization, fragmented markets, and a large total addressable market in the US. That night, I applied to every company of that type in the area.

## Getting the job

Three days in, barely anyone had replied, so I started showing up in person. I got three ride-along offers on the first day. Two converted to job offers. I accepted a role at a subsidiary of one of the biggest groups in the country.

Getting licensed isn't a formality. It involves book study, seminars, a proctored exam, and enough supervised time to handle controlled products independently. Most companies take two to three months to ramp a new technician, paying them the whole time.
