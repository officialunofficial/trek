Six months ago, our onboarding completion rate sat at an uncomfortable thirty-eight percent. Half of the people who signed up never made it to their first meaningful action inside the product.

We knew the experience was rough, but we had been working around it for years. A redesign felt risky, a quick fix felt insufficient, and neither felt like a priority when other work was pulling us in more urgent directions.

Then the growth team ran the numbers. A ten-point lift in onboarding completion would translate into a significant revenue increase over the following year. Suddenly the priority was obvious.

## What We Changed

The old flow asked every new user to complete fourteen steps before they reached the product itself. Most of those steps were optional in the sense that you could skip them, but the interface treated them as required and the visual weight made them feel mandatory.

We cut the flow to four steps. We rewrote every piece of copy to focus on what the user wanted to accomplish, not what we wanted them to tell us. We moved almost every preference and configuration into settings, reachable later.

## What Happened

Completion jumped from thirty-eight percent to sixty-one percent in the first month after the launch. Time to first meaningful action dropped by roughly half. Support tickets about the signup experience fell dramatically.
